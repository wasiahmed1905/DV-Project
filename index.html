01

The Rosling Race

Every dot is a country. Sliding right means richer; rising up means longer lives. Press play and watch the world climb the staircase, faster in some places than others.

x-axis: GDP per capita, inflation-adjusted USD (log scale). y-axis: life expectancy at birth (years). Bubble area: population. Colour: continent. Bubbles are drawn back-to-front by population so smaller countries stay legible.

02

The Choropleth

The same story, painted on the globe. Toggle the metric. Drag the year slider above. Click a country to pin it across every chart on this page.

Equirectangular projection. Sequential colour scale anchored to the metric’s global range across all years, so colours are comparable as you scrub through time.

03

Trajectories

Pin a handful of countries and trace their path through wealth-and-health space from 1952 to 2007. Some lines arc gracefully. Some stall. A few collapse and recover.

Each line is a country’s 12-point path. Start markers are hollow; end markers are filled. Dots between are decennial reference points. Pin countries above or click them on any chart.

04

The Inequality Swarm

For the selected year, every country is a circle, packed by life expectancy, split by continent. Tight clusters mean a region is converging. Long tails mean it isn’t.

Force-based beeswarm with continent rows. Radius encodes population (cube root scaled). Continent medians are drawn as vertical ticks.

05

The Bump Chart

Who climbed the rankings, and who slipped? The 20 most populous countries, ranked on the chosen metric across the seven decades.

Rank 1 = highest in the chosen metric for that year. Lines that cross are countries that overtook each other. Hover a row to read the country’s full trajectory.

06

The Trellis

One panel per country, three lines per panel: wealth, health, headcount. Patterns the headline charts cannot resolve become obvious here.

Each panel is a 12-point series, normalised to its own min-max within the panel so shapes are comparable across countries of very different scales. Colour encodes the metric, not the continent.
